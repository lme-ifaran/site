MARKOV CHAINS AS SUCCESSION MODELS: NEW PERSPECTIVES OF THE CLASSIC PARADIGM

D.O. Logofet
Lesovedenie, 2010, №2, pp.46-59 (in Russian)

Summary

Markov chains represent a simple kind of discrete-state random process and serve as a pertinent tool to formally describe a course of succession when the chain states are identified with certain stages of succession and a scheme of transitions among those stages is known. Data on stage durations and the likelihood of alternative transitions can be transformed to estimation of the transition probability matrix, and an immanent property of the absorbing chains, such as the convergence to a final stable distribution of its states, corresponds to the classic paradigm of succession theory: inevitable successive movement from pioneering stages to the stable (poly)climax one. The Markov model enables hereafter certain estimates of climax attainability times for various initial states and a proper probability distribution in the case of several climax states. Modern views of the forest ecosystem steady-state as of dynamical mosaic of newly formed and permanent ly overgrowing gaps in the closed forest canopy, with full-term distributions along the species and age dimensions, fit well the formalism of nonabsorbing chains, which suggests estimation of the relative area under various stages via the model steady-state vector. A new generation of Markov succession models - the time-inhomogeneous Markov chains - introduce some causality features into the pure phenomenological description which is typical of their homogeneous prototypes, thus responding the challenge to model long-term forest dynamics under the climate change.
