Current Publication

 

FOREST SUCCESSIONS IN THE CENTRAL AND EASTERN EUROPE: MARKOV-CHAIN MODELS AS A TOOL FOR LONG-TERM PREDICTIONS AND MANAGEMENT

EC project under INCO-Copernicus Program IC15-CT-98-0104
leader Prof. D.O. Logofet

Project dates        1998-2001

Summary

The Project was aimed at further development of scientific bases for sustainable management of forest resources through studying the current trends and predicting the future states in successional dynamics of particular forests typical for the Central and Eastern Europe. Three case studies of the Project represented three different levels of human impact on forest ecosystems, namely (see the map): 1. A mountain forest (Eastern Erzgebirge, south-east Germany), former spruce forest plantations devastated by ‘acid’ rains and other anthropogenic pressures; 2. A broad-leafed-coniferous forest under controllable cutting and renewal strategies of management (Russkii Les Forestry, Moscow Region, Russia); 3. A broad-leaved forest under reserve conditions (Bryanskii Les Reserve, Bryansk Region, Russia). New original methods have been proposed for construction Markov chains as models of vegetation successions and for applications of GIS technologies in geobotanic mapping and ecological risk assessment of the corresponding territories. Conceptual schemes of successions have been developed for the case studies at the current state of knowledge and with regard to the data available. The corresponding Markov chains have been constructed and studied mathematically (to the extent of non-absorbing, non-homogeneous and even non-Markov chains), calibrated and verified on the data by means of the modern GIS technologies. Model forecasts for the course and outcome of the successions are obtained in the terms of relative area distributions for the terminal succession stages and the average times to attain those stages. It has been shown that, in the long-term forest dynamics, the major risk is due to losses in biodiversity under anthropogenic pressure. In long-exploited forest, even the absolute reserve conditions appear to be ecological risk factors and need to be supplemented with certain actions to conserve biodiversity. An ecologically sound strategy of forest management, as a principal alternative to monoculture(spruce)-targeted forestry, has been shown to be, at least, compatible with the economic paradigm of forestry. Reforestation on the devastated area turns out to be in a crucial dependence upon certain actions to stimulate the early stages of forest succession, highly vulnerable to disturbances, even of natural origin.
